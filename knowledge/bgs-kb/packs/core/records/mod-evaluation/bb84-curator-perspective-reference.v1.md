# BB84's BGS curator perspective — one curator's lens, not a universal rubric

This record distills BB84's personal BGS curation perspective. It is published here
as a REFERENCE for curators who want to learn from one experienced approach. It is
NOT a KB rule and MUST NOT be used as objective evaluation criteria for any
modpack. Curators with different style intent — tactical, pure-aesthetic, comedy,
total-conversion, anything — are equally valid and should ignore the subjective
content here.

## Why BGS games matter to BB84

BB84's Bethesda lens starts from persistence. These games matter because the world keeps running without the player staring directly at it: NPCs have schedules, objects retain state, quests remember choices, and places become different because the player touched them. The product is not only story content; it is control over a stateful world.

He frames player agency through three linked rights: 控制权, the ability to alter world state; 反馈权, the ability to receive systemic response; and 记忆权, the guarantee that the world remembers the change. A good Bethesda mod should strengthen those rights. Modding is therefore co-creation, not merely patch-and-fix. The curator is helping author a version of the world.

## BB84's style north star

His default style is 原汁原味: vanilla fidelity as the anchor, not vanilla limitation as a cage. He prefers lore-friendly increments over revolutionary overhaul, RP depth over spectacle, and stability for long sessions over screenshot-first visual upgrade. A mod that looks spectacular but destabilizes a hundred-hour save is a bad trade for this style.

In Fallout 4 terms, this makes him anti-tactical, anti-COD, and anti-塔科夫 as a default pack direction. He is not claiming those styles are invalid. He is saying they do not serve his chosen world. His preferred aesthetic is cozy, atmospheric, lived-in, and systemically coherent rather than flashy, hyper-militarized, or pure showcase.

## Strengths to preserve

BB84 wants to preserve emergent system play: AI packages, factions, leveled lists, settlement or ship systems, inventory logic, quest stages, and stateful world feedback. He treats the FormID + Papyrus + quest-state graph as the real medium of Bethesda modding. A mod that participates in that graph expands the game; a mod that bypasses it often creates content that feels disconnected after the first trigger.

He also treats the mod platform itself as design intent. Bethesda games endure because players can become co-builders. Framework-friendly, patchable, extensible mods fit this culture better than closed set pieces that cannot be integrated.

## Weaknesses to compensate for

His lens is not blind fandom. He expects curators to compensate for Bethesda weaknesses: facial animation, destruction physics, shallow combat feel, and — for Starfield especially — repeated content structures. The point is not to pretend these weaknesses do not exist; it is to improve them without flattening the state-driven world underneath.

## BB84's implicit fit test

When BB84 asks whether a mod fits, he is asking: does it respect state-driven feedback, or does it override and flatten it? Does the world keep running with this content installed, or does the mod create scripted events that collapse after one trigger? Does it expand system space — rules, behaviors, distributions, relationships — or only scripted space? Does it support co-builder culture through compatibility, frameworks, and patchability?

Those questions are subjective because they serve his declared style. They become useful to other curators only after translation: declare your own style first, keep the objective safety rules, then decide which subjective world you are trying to build.
